## The Canadian Story

The commercial mackerel fishery in North America dates back to the 1600s. For four centuries, Atlantic mackerel was simply part of life on the Canadian coast — so abundant it was called *"the people's fish,"* catchable from shore with no boat, no licence, no expensive gear required.

Then came the foreign fleets. Between 1970 and 1976, Soviet and European factory ships pushed total catches of mackerel upward of 240,000 tonnes, reaching a maximum of nearly **420,000 tonnes in 1973 — over 925 million pounds in a single year.** Canada and the US responded by extending their 200-mile exclusive economic zones and expelling the foreign fleets. The fish began to recover. During the 1980s and 1990s, Canadian landings stabilized around 22,000 tonnes per year. Canadian landings reached a record high of **55,726 tonnes in 2005.**

Then the second collapse began — quieter, slower, and entirely domestic. The spawning stock biomass fell from a high of **386,314 tonnes in 1990** to a historic low of **27,599 tonnes in 2020.** That's a 93% collapse in the adult breeding population in thirty years. There have been no notable recruitment events since 2015 — meaning almost no young fish entering the population to replace what's being lost.

In 2022, the Minister of Fisheries closed the commercial and bait fisheries entirely. In 2025, the only permitted fishery is a **440-tonne bait-only allocation** — fish that cannot be sold commercially. DFO's rebuilding plan sets a goal of moving mackerel out of the critical zone in **18 years** — meaning approximately **2042.**

### The Canadian Punch Line
> *A fishery that produced 925 million pounds in 1973 now produces essentially zero — and won't recover until at least 2042.*

## The American Story

Atlantic herring has been fished along the US coast for centuries. But the modern industrialized story begins in 1960 with the arrival of Soviet vessels on Georges Bank. As additional countries entered the fishery, landings peaked at **470,000 metric tonnes in 1968 — over a billion pounds of fish, from a single bank off Cape Cod, in a single year.**

The high level of exploitation by multinational fleets, combined with poor recruitment, resulted in catastrophic decline. By 1977, just **2,127 tonnes** were landed from Georges Bank. The stock had collapsed. Between 1978 and 1985, virtually no adult or larval herring were detected on Georges Bank at all.

The stock slowly rebuilt over two decades of restraint. By the 2000s, the US herring fishery was functional again — not at its former glory, but productive. Then history repeated itself. Poor recruitment, warming waters, and continued fishing pressure began grinding the stock back down. The 2024 stock assessment indicated the need for an almost **90% reduction** from the previously projected 2025 quota of 23,961 tonnes — down to just **2,710 tonnes.** The 2025 allowable catch is the lowest in the entire history of the fishery management plan. Scientists project only a **50% probability of rebuilding by 2031.**

### The American Punch Line
> *The US went from a billion pounds on Georges Bank to a quota so small it barely supplies a regional bait shed — and the best-case recovery is still six years away.*

## The Combined Story — One Slide, One Paragraph

*"For generations, Atlantic herring and mackerel were the engine of the North American bait supply — billions of pounds annually, supporting a billion-dollar lobster industry and countless coastal communities. Two collapses, decades apart, have reduced both fisheries to near zero. The moratoriums are in place. The rebuilding plans are written. The recovery timelines stretch to 2031 at the earliest, 2042 at the latest. The lobster boats still need bait. The processing plants still need raw material. The demand hasn't gone anywhere. The supply has. That vacuum is what we're here to fill."*

## Talking Points for David Arenburg

- The supply gap is not a temporary blip. It is a **structural, multi-decade absence** with legally mandated timelines.
- The lobster industry — a **$1B+ annual fishery** — depends on herring and mackerel as bait. That demand is not going away.
- Every lobster boat on the Atlantic coast is currently sourcing substitute bait, much of it Pacific mackerel imported from Asia at inferior quality and $1.40/lb vs $2.40/lb for Atlantic species.
- The window to fill this gap is **6 to 17 years.** After that, domestic stocks will recover and the opportunity closes.
- Nicaragua can produce Atlantic-equivalent pelagics on the Caribbean side at **$0.35–0.50/lb production cost** — a margin of $0.90–$1.90/lb before any value-add.
- With David's Canadian Solar infrastructure, fishmeal production costs drop to **$600–900/tonne** vs a global benchmark of **$1,992/tonne.**
- This is not a speculative bet. The supply gap is documented, legally enforced, and measured in decades.
